The question the SAT itself got wrong

In May 1982, a geometry question appeared on the SAT. Every one of its five answer choices was wrong — including the one the answer key called correct. Try it, then see why provable reasoning succeeds where pattern-matching fails.

The problem. The radius of circle A is 1/3 the radius of circle B. Circle A rolls around circle B, without slipping, back to its starting point. How many complete revolutions does circle A make?

Pick the answer you think is correct.

The trap The "obvious" answer is 3 — circle B's circumference is 3× circle A's, so A should roll 3 times. That was the answer key's choice, and it's the one large language models pick too. Wrong.

The proof A circle rolling around another also revolves once from travelling the loop itself — the coin-rotation paradox. The true count is (RB/RA) + 1 = 3 + 1 = 4. Provably 4.

Four wasn't even a choice. Three students proved the question defective and roughly 300,000 SATs were rescored. The lesson we build on: the confident, pattern-matched answer (3) is a trap — only reasoning that models the actual geometry gets 4. That is what a deterministic verifier does, and what a probabilistic model, however fluent, does not.
